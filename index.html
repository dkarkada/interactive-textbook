What is this?

My goal here is to write a conceptual guide to astronomy and astrophysics aimed at the high-school level. I hope it’s accessible to people who are learning about astronomy for the first time; simultaneously, I hope it’s still interesting and useful to those of you who’ve had previous exposure.

\[E=h\nu=\frac{hc}{\lambda}\]

To accommodate a wide range of prior skill level, I’m experimenting with a new reading format. Sections are color-coded to represent the intended reading level. Intermediate and advanced information is collapsed by default, but can be expanded by toggling the triangular icon:

A light blue bar means intermediate-level content. Click the bar to collapse it again.

Dark blue means advanced content.

You can also pre-select your reading level at the top of each page, which auto-expands the relevant sections.

When I want to mention something in passing or remind you of something, I use footnotes. Hover over the softly underlined textThis is a footnote! to read the footnote. Hopefully this makes it easier to read useful comments without having to go back and forth between the footer and the main text.

I try to explain things in conceptual terms first, getting to the details later. So even if you consider yourself an advanced reader, I recommend reading through all the sections. It makes the reading experience more fluid and reinforces central concepts in your mental map of astrophysics.

Every so often, I put in some flashcards to review. Click to go through the set. You can ignore them if you want. Of course, it’s better if you don’t.

What do the colored bars represent?
Reading level. Light gray is beginner, light blue is intermediate, and dark blue is advanced.
How do you view footnotes?
Hover over the softly underlined text.

This resource was developed with the Science Olympiad Astronomy event in mind. The information here comes mainly from An Introduction to Modern Astrophysics by Carroll and Ostlie. I pulled some stuff from astrophysics classes I’ve taken (both undergraduate and graduate level).
